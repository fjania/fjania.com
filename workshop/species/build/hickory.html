Hickory

Carya spp. — Eastern North America
Janka Hardness
1,820 lbf
Density
0.72 sg (extremely dense)
Origin
Eastern North America
Grain
Typically straight, often wavy or irregular
About This Wood
Appearance
Heartwood: Light to medium brown with reddish undertones
Sapwood: Creamy white to pale tan; strong contrast with heartwood
Texture: Medium to coarse
Lustre: Moderate natural sheen
Why Choose It
  • Exceptional strength & impact resistance
  • Dramatic visual character (heartwood/sapwood contrast)
  • Outstanding wear resistance
  • Legendary durability for high-use items
  • Domestic availability and sustainability
Common Uses
  • Cutting boards & butcher blocks
  • Cabinetry & furniture
  • Tool handles & mallets
  • Stair treads & flooring
  • Workbenches & shop fixtures
  • Tabletops & heavy-use surfaces
Workshop Guide
Working Properties
Workability: Challenging but manageable with sharp tooling
Turning: Good results with sharp tools; dramatic grain shows well
Gluing: Excellent when pre-drilled for fasteners
Finishing: Accepts stains and clear coats well
Watch Out For
  • Requires sharp tooling at all times
  • Can tear out with aggressive passes
  • Difficult to work with dull blades
  • Significant density variation between boards
Finishing Tips
  • Use sharp blades — dull tools cause tear-out
  • Sand progressively to 220+ grit
  • Oil finishes highlight the grain contrast
  • Pre-drill for all fasteners
Buying Tips
  • Select kiln-dried stock for stability
  • Verify careful milling
  • Expect dramatic colour variation board-to-board
  • Calico (mixed heartwood/sapwood) adds character

Frequently Asked Questions
Is Hickory too hard to work by hand?
It's challenging but not impossible — keep tools sharp and take light passes.
Hickory vs Maple for cutting boards?
Both excellent. Hickory adds dramatic visual contrast; Maple offers a cleaner look.
Does Hickory stain well?
Yes, but test first — the sapwood and heartwood absorb stain differently.
